The BCS Oil Story

The BCS oil field was discovered in November 1953 with the drilling of wells BCS0006 and BCS0007. At that time the operating company decided to test those wells for an extended period of time in order to capture the most of relevant reservoir data. The producing formation is a sandstone /shale rock sequence of the Eocene age and have been identified in 7 different reservoirs separated by geological faults, impermeable rocks, and fluid contacts.

Commercial production began in 1971 when well BCS0018 was drilled. This proved the real potential of the reservoirs. A total of 44 producing wells were drilled in the field.

The water production in the Eastern part of the field began in September 1987, specifically in well BCS0031. Today’s water cut (the ratio of water in total fluids) is about 75%. In 2007 a water injection pilot project was conducted in a neighbor well to BCS0031, causing a production of injection water in BCS0031. This was the first time that professionals thought BCS could be a naturally fractured field.

The principal characteristics are a) The presence of an active aquifer and, b) Some reservoirs are partially naturally fractured.

The main average properties of the oil fields are:
Depth: 9500 feet
API gravity: 21°
Porosity: 7%
Permeability: 4 md Driven mechanism: Hydraulic, Solution gas
Lifting methods: Gas-lift, sucker rod beam pumping, electro-submersible pumping, progressive cavity pumping

Traditionally, proved reserves have been estimated by methods such as Arp’s DCA (Decline Curve Analysis) which consist of adjusting a straight line in a Log(Qo) vs. time plot and extrapolated it to an economic constrain. Such DCA methods based only in an empirical sense of variable decline such as pressure and rates are commonly used due to its simplicity.
On the other hand, more robust methodologies such as reservoir numerical simulation are difficult to implement in a short period of time due to the fractured character of the reservoirs and a lot of uncertainties in many variables. There is not enough data to do a fractured characterization.
The development reservoir department has decided to test technologies from machine learning to fit a model and make more robust forecasts of fluids (oil, water and gas) coming from the reservoirs. The company has focused specifically in multiple linear regression and neural networks (Deep learning) given today’s available libraries in several programming languages. So, we built a machine learning model in order to achieve a reasonable fit using the available data and make some predictions in short time constraint.

Reservoir: Porous and permeable rock unit with well-defined boundaries containing commercial amounts of hydrocarbons.
Well: A drilled cased borehole communicating the reservoir with surface installations. It’s a conduit.
